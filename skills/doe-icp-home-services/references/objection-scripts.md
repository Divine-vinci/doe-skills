# Objection Scripts

Eight common objections Mike will raise during sales calls or email replies. Each script includes WHY he's saying it (the subtext) and exactly how to respond.

## "I've been burned before by marketing guys"

**Why he says it**: He paid someone $1,000-$3,000 and got nothing. Maybe an SEO agency that promised page 1 rankings. Maybe a website that looked terrible.

**Response**:
> "That's exactly why I built the demo first — before asking for anything. You can see the actual website. If you like it, we move forward. If not, no hard feelings."

**The principle**: Demo-first selling removes the entire "will it work?" objection because he can SEE it before paying anything.

---

## "How much does this cost?"

**Why he says it**: He's price-comparing in his head. He's been quoted $3,000-$10,000 by agencies and expects to hear a scary number.

**Response**:
> "$500 gets you this exact website, live on your domain, within 3 days. No monthly fees, no contracts. You own it."

**If he balks at $500**:
> "What were you thinking? I might be able to adjust the scope."

**Floor pricing**: $300 for first 2 clients (to get testimonials). Never go below $300 — it signals desperation and attracts bad clients.

---

## "I already have a website"

**Why he says it**: He does — it's just terrible and he knows it.

**Response**:
> "I saw it — that's actually what prompted me to reach out. [Specific problem from brief]. The demo I sent is what a modern version could look like. Worth comparing?"

**The principle**: Lead with a specific issue. Show the contrast. Let him decide his current site isn't working.

---

## "I don't need a website, I get all my work from referrals"

**Why he says it**: Referrals ARE his main channel and he's proud of it.

**Response**:
> "Referrals are great — but when someone gets your name from a friend, the first thing they do is Google you. If your site looks outdated, they might call the next guy instead. A good site makes referrals stick."

**The principle**: Don't fight the referral pride. Reinforce it — your site makes his referral engine BETTER.

---

## "Can't I just use Wix/Squarespace/GoDaddy?"

**Why he says it**: He's heard of these tools and they're cheap or free.

**Response**:
> "You absolutely can. But most plumber sites on those platforms load in 6-8 seconds and look generic. The one I built for you loads in under 2 seconds, has click-to-call built in, and is designed specifically to convert visitors into phone calls. That's the difference between a website and a website that works."

**The principle**: Acknowledge the alternative exists. Then explain the specific, tangible difference.

---

## "I need to think about it"

**Why he says it**: He's interested but not urgent. Or he needs to check his cash flow. Or his wife handles the money.

**Response**:
> "Totally understand. The demo will stay live for the next couple weeks — take another look when you have a minute. If you have any questions, just reply to this email."

**Follow-up in 48 hours**: Short, different angle. Don't repeat the same pitch.

**The principle**: Don't push. Let the demo work on him. People who need to think about it often come back if the offer was clear.

---

## "I don't have time for this"

**Why he says it**: He's on a job site and genuinely busy.

**Response**:
> "I get it — you've got pipes to fix. Here's the short version: I already built the site. If you want it live, I handle everything. You send me your logo and hours, I do the rest. Total time from you: 10 minutes."

**The principle**: Quantify the time commitment. Make it tiny. Show you respect his schedule.

---

## "My nephew/friend built my current site"

**Why he says it**: He feels loyal and doesn't want to hurt someone's feelings.

**Response**:
> "Nothing wrong with that — but your business has probably grown since then. A site that worked 3 years ago might not be converting as well today. The demo shows what a 2026 version looks like. No pressure to switch — just thought you'd want to see the comparison."

**The principle**: Don't badmouth the nephew. Frame it as "business has evolved" not "your nephew sucks."

---

## Universal Objection Rules

1. **Never argue.** If he says "no," say "I understand. If anything changes, the demo will still be live."
2. **Never pressure.** Mike hates pushy sales people — it triggers his "marketing guy scam" memory.
3. **Always have a fallback.** "If the full price is too much, I can do a simpler version for less."
4. **Close with dignity.** Losing a sale gracefully keeps the door open for future referrals.
5. **Document objections.** Every "no" tells you something about your pitch. Use the Karpathy loop to improve.
